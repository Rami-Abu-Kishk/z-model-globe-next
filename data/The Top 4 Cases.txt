The Top 4 Cases:
1) The Russia–Ukraine Conflict (Region: Eastern Europe | Status: Critical):
This case represents a prolonged, high-intensity interstate war between Russia and Ukraine, driven by competing strategic objectives over territory, security influence, and geopolitical alignment. The conflict has evolved into a hybrid war combining conventional military operations, cyber warfare, economic sanctions, and information campaigns. The United States and NATO play a central role through military aid, intelligence support, and economic pressure on Russia, while European Union countries act as key financial, humanitarian, and energy stakeholders. The war’s global relevance stems from its impact on energy systems (especially gas flows to Europe), agricultural exports (notably Ukrainian grain), and defense spending cycles worldwide. It is classified as “critical” due to its persistence, escalation risks (including unconventional warfare), and its systemic influence on global inflation, supply chains, and geopolitical polarization.
2) Protests Against President Donald Trump’s Domestic Policies (Region: United States | Status: Warning):
This case reflects a structurally significant but contained domestic political tension within the United States, centered around policy decisions and political dynamics associated with Donald Trump. The situation is characterized by large-scale protests, legal disputes, and ideological polarization across federal and state levels, involving government institutions, opposition parties, advocacy groups, and segments of civil society. Unlike geopolitical conflicts, the impact is primarily internal, affecting governance continuity, regulatory direction, and social cohesion. Key stakeholders include domestic voters, businesses sensitive to policy shifts (taxation, trade, regulation), and international investors monitoring political stability in the world’s largest economy. Despite its visibility, the case remains in a “warning” category due to the resilience of U.S. institutional frameworks, though it still carries moderate risks to market sentiment, capital flows, and policy predictability.
3) Iran–Israel–US War: Tensions Threaten Regional Stability (Region: Middle East | Status: Critical):
This case represents a multi-layered, high-intensity geopolitical confrontation centered on the strategic rivalry between Iran and Israel, with the United States acting as a decisive military and strategic backer of Israel while also maintaining a direct security presence across the Gulf. The conflict operates across both direct deterrence (missile threats, airstrikes, cyber warfare) and indirect proxy arenas, where Iran leverages allied non-state actors across Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, and Yemen to expand its regional influence and pressure Israeli and U.S. interests. Core stakeholders include Gulf economies such as United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia, which face spillover risks to energy infrastructure, maritime security, and investor confidence, as well as global powers concerned with nuclear proliferation and freedom of navigation. The strategic significance of chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz amplifies the economic stakes, as any escalation could disrupt a substantial share of global oil and LNG flows. Overall, the case is classified as “critical” due to its high probability of rapid escalation, multi-domain warfare dynamics, and systemic implications for global energy markets, supply chains, and regional security architecture.


4) Israel–Lebanon Conflict: Dimensions of Tension and Regional Implications (Region: Middle East | Status: Critical):
This case represents a volatile and asymmetric conflict between Israel and Lebanon, primarily driven by military confrontations between Israeli forces and Hezbollah. The conflict operates within a broader regional power struggle, where Iran provides strategic, financial, and military backing to Hezbollah, positioning Lebanon as a key front in Iran’s deterrence strategy against Israel. The dynamics include cross-border missile exchanges, targeted strikes, and the constant risk of escalation into full-scale war. Stakeholders extend beyond the immediate actors to include the United States and European powers engaged in diplomatic containment, as well as regional economies exposed to security instability in the Eastern Mediterranean. The classification as “critical” reflects the high escalation potential, the involvement of state and non-state actors, and the broader implications for regional security, energy infrastructure, and investor risk perception.
