The Economic and Investment indicators descriptions
Economic Indicators:
1. Trade Volume
Trade volume is an economic indicator that measures the total value of goods and services exchanged between countries over a specific period of time. This includes exports and imports of goods such as industrial products, raw materials, and energy, as well as services like transportation, tourism, and finance. This indicator is important because it reflects the level of economic openness and integration between markets, as well as the strength of trade relations and global supply chains. A rise in trade volume typically indicates expanding economic activity and stronger demand, while a decline suggests a slowdown in economic activity or the presence of trade restrictions.

2. GDP Growth
GDP growth is an economic indicator that measures the rate of change in the total value of goods and services produced within an economy over a specific period of time. It reflects the extent of economic expansion and productivity and is widely used as a primary measure of economic health and growth potential. GDP growth is influenced by factors such as consumption, investment, government spending, and external trade, as well as technological and demographic changes. An increase in this indicator signals economic expansion, higher employment, and improved corporate profits, while a decline points to economic slowdown or weaker overall demand.

3. Inflation Rate
The inflation rate is an economic indicator that measures the continuous change in the general level of prices of goods and services over time, reflecting changes in the purchasing power of money. Inflation is influenced by multiple factors such as production costs, energy and food prices, demand levels, and monetary policies. It is a key measure of economic stability, as higher inflation reduces purchasing power and increases the cost of living, while low or stable inflation reflects a more balanced economic environment. Moderate inflation is often considered healthy as it indicates sustained demand and economic growth.







4. Unemployment Rate
The unemployment rate is an economic indicator that measures the percentage of individuals within the labor force who are able and willing to work but are unable to find employment. It reflects the condition and efficiency of the labor market in generating job opportunities relative to the supply of labor. The unemployment rate is influenced by economic cycles, growth levels, industrial structure, and technological changes. A high unemployment rate typically indicates weak economic activity and reduced labor demand, while a low rate suggests a strong economy and higher job creation, although excessively low levels may lead to wage pressures.
Investment Indicators:
1. Global Investment as % of GDP:
It is an indicator that measures the size of investment relative to gross domestic product over a given period. It shows the share of output allocated to capital formation, such as spending on infrastructure, buildings, machinery, equipment, and other productive assets. It is used to assess the extent to which an economy is directed toward expanding its future productive capacity.
2. Global Investment Growth (%):
It is an indicator that measures the rate of change in investment over time, usually from one year to the next. It shows whether investment has increased or decreased, and by what percentage. It is used to understand the direction of investment activity and the degree to which it is accelerating or slowing over time.
3. Global FDI Inflows as % of GDP:
It is an indicator that measures the value of foreign direct investment flows during a given period relative to gross domestic product. It reflects the importance of cross-border productive investment in an economy, such as establishing new projects, acquiring influential ownership stakes in companies, or reinvesting earnings in existing operations. It is used to assess the role of FDI in economic activity.
4. Global External Debt as % of GDP:
It is an indicator that measures the size of external debt relative to gross domestic product. External debt includes financial obligations owed to nonresidents, such as loans, bonds, and other liabilities. This indicator is used to assess the extent to which an economy depends on external financing and to monitor debt vulnerability and repayment capacity.
5. Global Savings as % of GDP:
It is an indicator that measures the share of income or output that is not spent on consumption and is retained as saving during a given period. It shows the portion of resources available to finance investment or support financial stability. It is used to understand an economy’s capacity to generate internal resources for growth.

Outlook & Drivers
The global investment-to-GDP ratio is expected to rise gradually and steadily, without sharp acceleration, reflecting a slow structural increase in global capital formation driven by long-term economic transformation.
The main driver of this trend is sustained demand for capital-intensive investments in energy transition infrastructure, digital systems (data centers, semiconductors, AI capacity), water infrastructure, logistics networks, and defense-related industries. These sectors require continuous, multi-year investment, supporting a steady upward drift in global investment levels.
However, the pace remains constrained by tighter financial conditions, higher financing costs, and elevated debt levels across many economies, which limit the scope for a broad-based investment boom.
Overall, global investment is shifting from broad cyclical expansion to targeted, strategic allocation of capital, resulting in a stable but gradual increase in investment intensity rather than a sharp surge.

Investment growth is expected to be moderate in 2026, then gradually improve as financing conditions normalize and strategic spending shifts from the announcement phase to execution. In addition, the weak investment base highlighted by the IMF for 2026 amid global disruptions, UNCTAD’s warnings about fragile global investment, and the large scale of data center and energy expansion all support this pattern: weak in the near term, followed by stronger momentum toward the end of the decade.

Outlook & Drivers
Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) is expected to recover, but at a slower pace than total investment. This reflects structural factors, as global firms continue to invest, but a growing share of investment is taking place through domestic capital expenditure, reshoring to allied countries, state-led industrial subsidy programs, and investment structures that do not translate into traditional cross-border FDI as efficiently as in previous cycles.
According to the latest UNCTAD monitor, the rebound observed in 2025 overstated the true strength of underlying real investment activity. A significant portion of the increase was driven by conduit flows passing through financial centers, while actual investment projects—particularly greenfield investment and project finance—remained relatively weak.
As a result, FDI is expected to increase, but with a lower economic multiplier effect and a more fragmented and less concentrated pattern compared to earlier phases of globalization.

Outlook & Drivers
Global savings are expected to edge up slightly in line with investment, with no significant gap emerging at the global level. The main drivers include strong corporate cash generation in the technology and energy sectors, precautionary saving by governments and households amid geopolitical uncertainty, and demographic-driven excess savings in parts of Asia and Europe.
On the downside, fiscal dissaving—driven by higher spending on defense, climate adaptation, and debt servicing—acts as a limiting factor. Overall, the global savings rate is likely to increase only marginally, which also explains why the investment-to-GDP ratio in this outlook rises only gradually.
In addition, near-term IMF projections for growth and inflation, combined with elevated global debt pressures, reduce the likelihood of any sharp decline in global savings.
